Until recently, I’ve typically written code by hand, as was thoroughly engrained in me by my CS program. Shoutout to CU Boulder.

But I’ve noticed a lot of job applications now ask about experience with AI tools, so I decided a Mandarin Chinese learning app would be a fun project to “vibecode.” I set out prompting in Codex and started building.

Slowly, it’s taking shape as an app that I both build and use. That means I can keep iterating on the design even while it’s already a working product. Of course, that’s possible when coding by hand too, but with far fewer keystrokes, I’m able to spend more time thinking about the product and the user experience, and less time getting stuck in syntax.

I will say though, this technology has a long way to go. At one point during the project, I decided to type "restart the server" to see if Codex would be faster at it than me, what happened is that it uncovered that Codex had built multiple conflicting databases, made inconsistent changes, and had tried to restart the server on its own, and when it failed, it made unneeded changes to the environment, and failed to undo them. (Not too much unlike a freshman computer science student)
